Towards Real-Time Interactive Fluid Rendering

Daniel Brown1, Alex Coppens2,
1University of Maryland, 2University of Maryland
Paper arXiv Video Code Data

Abstract

Rendering interactive fluid in real-time has a number of applications in video-games, engineering visualization tools and animation. For many of these applications, approaches which trade physical accuracy for performance are acceptable so long as visual plausibility is retained. Existing approaches struggle to achieve real-time performance due to poor scaling and computational complexity. Our work investigates multiple possible approaches to real-time interactive fluid rendering and evaluates their feasibility and drawbacks. We describe an attempt to approximate fluid using a highly simplified particle based simulator. We outline two proposed neural network architectures for approximating fluid flow and evaluate their feasibility. We describe modifications made to an open-source SPH simulation tool meant to facilitate the generation of training data for these neural networks.

Introduction

Realtime physics engines such as Havok, Chaos for Unreal Engine, PhysX, and Unity's built-in engine are designed to produce plausible physical interactions while having sufficient performance to be interactable in real time. Most real-time engines implement rigid body dynamics using broad-phase and narrow-phase collision detection. These steps are then followed by impulse-based or constraint-based collision methods. For sparse materials, such as fire, smoke and rain, particle based simulations are used. Simulations of these materials often ignore inter-particle interactions for improved performance. Environmental elements such as fluid or soft bodies require specialized physically grounded simulation techniques such as Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH) and Position-Based Dynamics (PBD) respectively. These approaches feature a range of tradeoffs and limitations. SPH simulations suffer from poor scaling as the number of particles required to simulate a volume increases cubically as the particle radius decreases. SPH simulations spend much of their runtime in neighboring particle searches. For many commonly simulated fluids, such as water and gasoline, enforcing incompressibility is necessary for physical accuracy. Additionally, the time step size required to maintain the numerical stability of the simulation may be smaller than the frame rate of the rendering engine, requiring multiple time steps to be simulated per frame. Interactions between fluids and rigid bodies introduces additional complexity as these interfaces require special handling of pressure and boundary conditions. Within the context of game engines, frame rates must be high enough to meet user expectations and thus trading physical accuracy for performance is acceptable. Common simplifications made in engines for real-time fluid simulation include larger grids, extruded 2-d fluids and precomputed fluids. In order to advance real-time fluid simulation, we explored alternative simulation and machine-learning based solutions. We attempted to develop a custom simulation pipeline, applying multiple un-physical simplifications to quickly generate particle data suitable for fluid rendering. We realized that these simplifications resulted in numerical instability and were insufficient in reproducing visually plausible fluid effects, so we switched to adapting the open-source SPlisHSPlasH SPH simulator. We modified SPlisHSPlasH to produce ground-truth training data for data-driven modeling. We then explored a feed-forward neural network to learn time-dependent fluid evolutions via velocity field prediction. Due to severe dimensionality problems with this approach, we transitioned to investigating a Lagrangian Graph Neural Network (LGNN) architecture. We believe this would allow us to model fluid systems with more generality and apply model parallelism to faster training. It also offers a promising direction for efficient, high-fidelity fluid simulations beyond the constraints of traditional real-time physics engines.

Related Work

The challenges of simulating physics within real-time game engines have let to extensive research into both simulation and machine learning approaches. The work \cite{Braley2009FluidSF} Fluid Simulation for Computer Graphics: A tutorial in Grid-Based and Particle-Based Methods, outlines Eulerian and Lagrangian frameworks for fluid simulation which are commonly used for interactive graphics applications. To handle these complexities when it comes to soft bodies and computational efficiency, \cite{10.1145/3588432.3591521}Data-Free Learning of Reduced-Order Kinematics, introduces techniques that aim to reduce simulation complexity by getting compact representations in kinematic behaviors without extensive training datasets. Ubisoft research expands upon this with, \cite{10.1145/3309486.3340245}Subspace Neural Physics: Fast Data-Drive Interactive Simulation, which goes on to leverage subspace models and neural networks to accelerate simulations while maintaining a high frame rate within interactivity. This effectively bridges physics-based modeling and data-driven optimizations. With recent advances within the field of machine learning, neural network architectures have further expanded the possibilities for physically accurate, and computationally efficient, real-time simulations representations. The study \cite{bhattoo2022learningdynamicsparticlebasedsystems}Learning the Dynamics of Particle-based Systems with Lagrangian Graph Neural Networks (2022) specifically targets particle-based physical systems, and demonstrates how graph-based learning frameworks can capture complex particle iterations, which significantly improve simulation stability and generalization capabilities. There are also the theoretical connections explored within Connecting \cite{zhao2021connectinggraphconvolutionalnetworks} graph convolutional networks and graph-regularized PCA which provides insights into how graph structures can efficiently encode physical constraints and neighborhood interactions. By doing this, it enhances the interpretability and performance of learned physical methods. The above works collectively establish a foundation for our exploration of neural-network-driven fluid simulations, specifically when achieving accurate, interactive-scale fluid dynamics while addressing computational bottlenecks.

Proposed Approaches

Naive Solution

Fluid simulation methods can be separated into a few broad categories. \cite{fluids6120460} Eulerian simulations discretize space into a regular grid, with each grid cell containing fluid properties such as velocity and pressure at that location. Eulerian simulations are less favored for rendering applications due to their fixed resolution, and the difficulty of implementing fluid-air interfaces and droplets. Lagrangian simulations discretize fluid into a collection of particles with positions over a continuous simulation domain, hence the term smoothed particle hydrodynamics. The Lagrangian approach is more common in rendering applications because the particle discretization allows for easy replication of phenomena like droplets and splashes, and the lack of a fixed resolution results in an absence of grid artifacts. The open-source fluid simulation framework SPlisHSPlasH implements a Lagrangian fluid simulation based on SPH. It offers a selection of pressure solvers for implementing incompressibility, multiple particle kernel function options, and supports coupling with rigid and deformable bodies. SPlisHSPlasH uses additional libraries for fast rigid body collisions and particle neighborhood search.
